Why ten minutes

Georges St-Pierre — UFC welterweight champion. One of his core principles: he trained at an intensity he could sustain, so he could come back the next day. Not crushing one session and burning out. Returning. Daily.

That's our model. Ten minutes today beats sixty minutes on Saturday. Every time. The hand learns through return. The ear learns through return. Daily contact beats occasional intensity.

A Note on Streaks & The Return

Streaks celebrate consistency — they don't measure your worth. If a day or a week passes without the guitar, the move is simple: show up today. Yesterday is closed. Today is open. Music becomes part of life the way meals do — you don't shame yourself for skipping breakfast; you eat lunch. Same here.

The Anchor Trick · C ↔ Em

For C → Em: your middle finger doesn't move at all. It stays planted on the D string, fret 2. The ring finger lifts off, the index slides over to the A string. That single anchored finger is your pivot — the easiest chord change you'll learn. Start here.

The 5th-fret rule

The 5th fret of any string sounds the same as the open string above it. Exception: the G string uses the 4th fret to match the open B.

By-ear cross-checks

E string · fret 5= open A
A string · fret 5= open D
D string · fret 5= open G
G string · fret 4= open B ←
B string · fret 5= open e

⌁   The Mute Drill

As you get comfortable with the chord changes, dive into the rhythm separately. Rest your fretting hand lightly across the strings — no chord, just muting. Now practice the strum pattern alone, hearing the percussive chunk-chunk. Your strumming hand learns the groove without your fretting hand competing for attention. Then bring them back together.
